Callouts Evolved

Accessible microphone augmentation for gamers

  • Client
  • Deaf Destiny Network
  • Sector
  • Video Games, Accessibility
  • My Role
  • Project Manager, Designer, Software Developer
  • Project Time
  • Ongoing

Callouts Evolved is an online platform that anables cross cultural communication between gamers by providing a shared online augmentative and alternative communication grid. The core design is inspired by gotalk devices that allow individuals with autism to speak simple commands. The Deaf Destiny Network commissioned this work to make it easier for deaf players to get into and stay in groups for end game content.


Consume

The first step is to learn as much as you can about the situation, the problem at hand, and how you can best serve the client.

Clearly Defining the Problem

Let's paint a picture. Members of the Deaf Destiny Network reached out to me asking for more opportunities to participate in end game activities. In the gaming community you have options for who to run activities with, but to improve your odds of winning you want to pick stronger players. You can choose to play with other members in your clan (which typically meant waiting in a queue for a chance to play next month), or go to a Looking for Group site to join teams looking to run right now. In the Destiny Series communication is considered to be very important. This causes the average gamer to pick weak players with communication over very strong players without it. This difference in communication fueled 2 different methods of completing the same activities. Members would look for visual clues and know what visual clues trigger which events as well as enemy spawn patterns and the mechanics of the fight. Other gamers rely on verbal call outs from their team to know what is going on. These are two equally valid play styles but they do not blend well.

An interview in 2016 revealed that players who could not use audio to communicate had to try three time longer to get into and stay in groups when compared to other gamers. This disparity wastes time for all gamers involved but is caused by there being less opportunities available to players without audio communication. This project aims to increase awareness and thereby increase the number of opportunities while also providing a communication relay between gamers that will work regardless of their prefered methods. There are a few challenges though:

Challenge #1

Allow members to interact with other gamers without other gamers having to do extra work.

Challenge #2

Accomodate as many disabilities as possible, not just members.

Challenge #3

Encourage gamers to search for like minded teams before going through typical looking for group communities.


Enhance

After the problem is clearly defined we can look at how others have attempted to solve the problem and where they fell short. Through a shared experience you can start development on a more comprehensive solution.

Competitve Analysis

When this project was commisioned, the only widespread applications that were remotely comparable were Discord and Glide. Deaf users liked to use Glide to quickly sign their call outs, but this required the target players to be looking while the call out was made... which often put players at risk. This method also didn't help with cross cultural communication. Discord was the other alternative but the api had strict requirements to prevent abuse that often delayed text call outs. Today, both Microsoft and Playstation have released speech to text overlays for their respective consoles that allow hard of hearing gamers to see what was said. This is a great first step but it clearly does not address all the problems with cross cultural video game communication. Without a reliable communication relay, members of the network had to wait months in a queue for their turn to run with the teams that knew what to do and would take them, or spend days requesting to join groups and being denied, or getting in just to be kicked at the start of the game.

A chat room approach would be best to ensure that call outs are delivered to the appropriate groups. Speech to text can help players who are hard of hearing obtain more information than they would otherwise have access to, but it isn't the most accurate and requires some filtering to figure out what was said. This is best suited for casual conversations between games. Microsoft and Playstation have fairly accurate speech to text engines, but they are too slow, small, and contextual for gamers to react to i

Feature #1

Microsoft and Playstation are on the right track by providing speech to text for thier voice calls. Unfortunately, these are only really helpful for players who are hard of hearing and can quickly scan for the missing information. To enable actionable visual call outs for deaf users we will implement speech commands that can trigger visual call outs.

Feature #2

Text to speech has been implemented by Discord but it requires the user to input /tts before each message which takes too much time in game. Because of this, we will implement toggles for text to speech that can work independently between the normal chat and call outs.

Feature #3

When considering the speech to text and text to speech loop, we must recognize that not all of our users can trigger speech commands. To accomodate these users we must setup keyboard shortcuts. The number pad to the right of all tenkey keyboards is the easiest to learn but we must also incorperate the keyboard for laptop users. The soundboard was initially developed with pre-recorded call outs for a more natural experience, but have transitioned to tts for better compatibility.

Feature #4

With the soundboard implementation, we can change the order of call outs to mimic different devices and take on a form inspired by AAC boards. Triggering a call out by tap, click, type, or speech command will cause it to glow to give deaf users a visual indication of what is going on.

Feature #5

The above features would put Callouts Evolved on par with mic based communication, but we need an incentive to get more users on board. We can implement timers to synchronize player actions with in game events, and a history function so everyone can see the order in which call outs were made and who made them.

Feature #6

Lastly, to encourage groups to form from like minded individuals we can implement a built in looking for group chat.


Replicate

Finally, we can attempt to solve the problem through itteration, development, and testing.

Itteration

This is the project that introduced me to web development through PHP, Laravel, Websockets, Trello, Sourcecode, and Bitbucket. The prototype was built by James Stone at NomadJSDev by combining the features from my targeted research, discordjs bot, android app, windows executable, and wix cms blog. The project has since been shuttered but you can read their perspective on the project. Since then, I've rebuilt the site with HTML, CSS, Jquery, and Firebase Real Time Database. Go check out the 2020 version but be sure to come back.

The third iteration of the website is the currently live version that provides paid services and accepts donations through react-paypal-buttons-v2.

Feel free to explore either version of the website and use a test account while doing so: testing1@gmail.com Testing1

Results:

Jason Santos, the clan leader for BAM Optical Raiders, utilized an early prototype to get their entire team through a world's first pure deaf leviathan raid completion.

Since the release of the third version, hundreds of gamers have been able to complete their first activity and plenty of clans have started adopting Callouts Evolved as their default communication interface.
